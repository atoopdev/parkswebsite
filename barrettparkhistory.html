Barrett Park

A History of Barrett Park

In the commentary below, I have included a brief history of Barrett Park in West Hood River, and my experience addressing the issues of the Barrett Park RC flying field and the non-permitted rest area/open space with Hood River Valley Parks and Recreation District management and local elected officials

From Community Playing Fields to a Playground for RC Hobbyists: A Brief History of Barrett Park

After a 2003 countywide public opinion survey identified new playing fields as one of the top recreational needs in Hood River County, Hood River Valley Parks and Recreation District made development of new playing fields a top priority. The District's 2005 Capital Facilities Master Plan indicated that: "Hood River County has a critical shortage of public field space for baseball, soccer, and lacrosse." To address this need, the Parks District started searching for land.

The 2006 acquisition of the working orchard at 4010 Barrett Dr was partially funded by a $325,800 grant from Oregon State Parks, and occurred for the express purpose of providing additional playing fields for the Hood River community.

The problem was, the 32 acre orchard the District purchased isn't zoned for dedicated sports playing fields; it's zoned Exclusive Farm Use (EFU). Plus it's on a hill. In other words: not flat. Nevertheless, the District bought the property and commissioned an architectural site plan for a playing field complex that was mailed to county officials and adjacent property owners.

By 2011, Hood River Valley Parks and Recreation District had owned the Barrett property for over 3 years, received an additional $494,000 from the State Parks Department Local Government Grant program, and learned that building a ballfield complex on the property might be nearly impossible. Although playing fields had been identified as top priority for the District and were the reason for the initial purchase, the plan shifted to a multi-use park. A second site plan was commissioned and mailed out to interested parties.

Local land use oversight and community advocacy organization Thrive Hood River (Hood River Valley Resident's Committee) lobbied the District to sell the property and purchase a property not zoned as exclusive farm use/high value farmland (EFU/HVF). The alternate property they suggested is located on the corner of Fairview Rd and Belmont Drive, diagonally across from Westside School. However, the District remained committed to the Barrett property, despite the fact their $325,800 grant could have been redirected to a property with a better prognosis for development.

In winter 2011, the Parks District had the pear and apple trees ripped out with backhoes in order to "keep their grant."

In the face of a legal challenge to the project from a local farmer, the District proceeded to spend at least $85,500 of taxpayer money on attorneys and consultants to fuel years of appeals. All to develop a piece of agricultural land that no longer worked for playing fields.

In December 2013, the Hood River County Board of Commissioners rejected the District's application to for a conditional use permit to develop Barrett for a second time.

In 2014, over my strenuous objections, Hood River Valley Parks and Recreation District installed a 300' radio-controlled aircraft runway 85 feet from my property, and opened the property to drones and other RC aircraft from dawn to dusk, 365 days/year. The negative impact from this to neighbors has grown with each successive year.

85 feet?! How can this be? Aren't there rules and laws that protect neighboring property owners?

In 1997, a group of state legislators did a favor for one state senator's constituent: a wealthy Willamette Valley landowner. These legislators made flying fields for radio-controlled aircraft an outright allowed usage on EFU land. No permit needed. Perhaps they thought of EFU land as expansive acreage with no close neighbors. Perhaps it didn't cross their mind that anyone would be put an RC runway 85 feet from a neighboring property. Regardless, they included no protections for neighboring residents and property owners and no minimum distance RC flying fields must be located from occupied dwellings.

In subsequent years, a few attempts were made in the Oregon legislature to either repeal this change or introduce language meant to protect adjacent landowners/residents. All were stalled in committee.

A recent attempt to change the law came in 2021-22 when I contacted former State Representative Anna Williams (D-HD52) about introducing language into the Oregon Revised Statutes to impose restrictions on the distance an RC flying field can be located from an occupied residence.

You can read those emails here.

Rep. Williams ultimately did not respond to a follow-up email I sent in March 2022, and resigned from the Oregon Legislature in August 2022.

What about the County? Don't they have anything to say about a public RC flying field being located next to established residences? What about quality of life? Hood River is supposed to be a great place to live.

Hood River Valley Parks and Recreation District publicly advertises Barrett Park as a community park and open space, but they do not have a conditional use permit that allows them to use the property in this way. Non-authorized usage of the property occurs daily.

In 2019, I wrote to County Community Development Director Eric Walker whose office is responsible for enforcing Oregon land use laws in Hood River County about the non-authorized usage of the property.

You can read the questions I asked and Eric Walker's responses to them here.

In this exchange, Eric Walker confirms that Hood River Valley Parks and Recreation District does not have the conditional use permit required to operate the property as a community park and open space. Eric Walker then tells me that his office will not be enforcing Oregon land use law in this case.

Why does a paid county administrator get to decide whether or not to enforce Oregon land use law?

Ok, but why write to your State Representative and the County Commissioners? Can't this be worked out with Hood River Valley Parks and Recreation District?

Unfortunately, Hood River Valley Parks & Recreation District has straight up refused to take any steps to improve this situation despite many attempts on my part that have spanned nearly a decade. They have told me there must be compromise, but the compromise is expected to be mine only. Their idea of compromise is to finish a fence that was provided to all other neighbors years ago without contingencies. They also offered a "deal" in which I could buy trees and bushes to plant on the park property as a buffer for the north end of my property. I would be responsible for maintaining/watering this buffer, but Parks and Rec would own all the trees and bushes I bought. They have refused to consider all meaningful compromises.

  • They have refused limited hours for RC activities, telling me "All our parks are open dawn to dusk, 365 days/year."
  • They have refused to move the access road and parking area further away from me to address privacy and safety concerns.
  • They have refused to plant any trees or vegetation that I don't pay for.
  • They have refused to install a gate to keep the property closed at night.
  • They have refused to acknowledge or address the fact that their management of the property is not in compliance with Oregon land use law ORS 215.283, and continue to promote the property as a "community park."

Of the Parks Board members and management I have dealt with, only Mike McCarthy has shown compassion and been willing to engage with me in a respectful way about this situation that the District has imposed on my life. All other board members have refused to respond in any meaningful or constructive way to the issues I have raised or they have refused to respond at all. I was told by Director Mark Hickok that the Parks Board members are under no obligation to respond to me or engage with me, that it is the Director's job to deal with the public. This is wrong. These people are elected officials. They are not members of a corporate board.

Most recently, the Parks Board refused professional mediation. In November 2022, Mike McCarthy attempted to organize a mediation session through Six Rivers Mediation which would have been partially covered by a grant for disputes involving agricultural land. The balance of the expense would have been my responsibility: there would have been no cost to the District. On the advice of their attorney (paid for with Hood River County tax dollars) a majority of the Board refused mediation with me because they had not received a tort claim notice of my intent to pursue litigation. They did this in an executive session which is allowed by law to discuss pending lawsuits. They refused mediation because there was no lawsuit pending. I learned this from Mike McCarthy and the mediator from Six Rivers: neither the District management or their attorney bothered to notify me of this.

I have been told by an attorney how expensive litigation would be, and that there are no guarantees. The people who run the District know that I am a single, working person who is unable to afford expensive litigation without risking my financial wellbeing. Most of us can't afford litigation against a publicly funded municipal agency. They literally have the taxpayers bankrolling their defense. Do we really want a Parks District that will only respect us if we have the money to take them to court?"

The Takeaway: Building on failure is costly

Hood River Valley Parks and Recreation District received $819,800 dollars from Oregon State Parks to provide Hood River County with a new community recreational property.

However:

  • Hood River Valley Parks and Recreation District has failed to provide one single new playing field since the need was recognized in 2003. The community is still playing on the same playing fields available for use when I was a kid here.
  • Hood River Valley Parks and Recreation District has failed to provide a westside community park to a generation of Hood River's kids. The kids who were born when the Barrett orchard was purchased in 2006 are now 17 years old.
  • The $494,000 in grant dollars meant for the development of our westside community park was redirected to fund the development of the Golden Eagle Pump Track and ball courts.
  • In 2021, Hood River Valley Parks and Recreation District purchased the Fairview property for a westside community park. This is the same property diagonal to Westside Elementary School that Thrive Hood River lobbied for as an alternative to Barrett so many years ago. The District currently lacks the money to develop it.
  • Barrett Park is the "official flying field" of the Columbia Gorge Aeromodelers, a private RC club. Hood River County taxpayers are paying for the upkeep and maintenance of this property while Hood River Valley Parks and Recreation District provides it to the Columbia Gorge Aeromodelers free of charge. This is what I learned from Director Mark Hickok in 2019.
  • Hood River Valley Parks and Recreation District could have sold Barrett and rolled the grant into a comparable property when they failed to receive a conditional use permit in 2013.
  • They could have returned Barrett to farmland.
  • They could have prioritized building Hood River a community park.
  • Instead, the 17 year long history of Barrett Park is a story of inflexible egos and prioritizing special interests.
